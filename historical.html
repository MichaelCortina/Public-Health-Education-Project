The History of Quarantine

 Quarantines have been an effective strategy in disease prevention over the past centuries. The quarantine first originated in medieval Venice in the 1370s. As the Black Death rampaged across Europe, Venice sent incoming ships to wait 40 days on a nearby island if it was suspected to contain the plague (Klibanoff 2020). This system used by Italian Cities was later adopted by other European Countries (Tognotti 2013).

 While the form and time period of the quarantine has changed the strategy has been repeated to great effect in preventing disease ever since. Another successful example of historical quarantining was Haiti's avoidance of the Cholera pandemic. While neighboring colonized islands were ravaged by Cholera spread from imported soldiers and slaves Haiti managed to prevent any major cholera outbreak throughout the 19th century. By implementing strict quarantine policies on incoming ships, and disallowing access to ships from infected islands Haiti prevented disease vectors from reaching the island (Jenson and Szabo 2011).
